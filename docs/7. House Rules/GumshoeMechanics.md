# Drives
 All GUMSHOE characters have a Drive, an overriding goal or personality trait that propels him into adventures and mysteries. Choose one of the Drives listed below for your character. Drives do not make characters suicidally reckless, and do not wholly define the character’s personality, but they ensure that characters enthusiastically pursue investigations and are spurred to action instead of being overly cautious. Drives can also serve as plot hooks for the GM to tailor adventures to the characters.
## Adventure

You’re the sort who taunts  dragons and leaps into mysterious swirling portals of potential doom. Adventure is in your blood, and you’ll seek it out wherever your wandering feet take you. ambition You have designs on high office; you want to be a noble, an important official, or a power behind some throne. To bring these plans to fruition, you need allies, influence, and power. Every case closed is a favour owed to you; you see adventures as stepping stones to your ultimate goal.  
## Altruism 
You are a defender of the common people, of the little folk. In a world of grave dangers and deadly monsters, you are the shield that protects and the light that drives back the darkness. You seek to bring peace and justice to all good folk, and ask little in return. atonement Your past is a litany of shameful or even horrific deeds. Perhaps you were a thief, a brigand, or something worse, like a diabolist or blackguard. You walked a very dark road, but now you seek atonement and redemption. You want to erase the sins of your past and clear your name. 
## Arcana 
Magic burns in your blood. You hunger for more arcane power – spells, magical items, or occult secrets lost for aeons. The whiff of brimstone or the rumour of sorcery is enough to pique your curiosity. You might be a trained wizard or a sorcerer, or just have an obsession with all things arcane and magical. Any mystery involving magic is irresistable to you. 
## Chronicler 
The ages of the world turn like spokes on some great wheel, and who will remember the events of the past if they are not recorded? You are driven by a desire to chronicle events great and small, to record the truth of such matters for the illumination of future generations. You might follow a specific band of heroes, recording their great deeds in saga or song, or seek to write the comprehensive history of your times. If you are to write the true account of this troubled world, you must learn all its secrets... 
## Compulsion 
You are compelled by some external force to seek adventure and mysteries. This could be an oath, a magical geas or curse, or maybe even you were made for this purpose by some god or mad alchemist. In any event, you cannot resist this drive; it is engraved on your very soul. 
## Curiosity 
You have a condition that’s often fatal to adventurers. In a dungeon, curiosity can get you killed, but you can’t resist the urge to pull mysterious levers, open sealed caskets, and drink unlabelled potions. The idea of leaving a puzzle unfinished is anathema to you – better to risk your life finding the truth than to endure the niggling feeling that comes when you leave an interesting mystery unsolved. 
## Duty 
You are honour-bound to bring justice to the world. This could be your actual profession, so you could be a wandering magistrate, a bounty hunter, a champion for hire or a private detective. Alternatively, you may have sworn an oath or feel an overriding sense of duty to the cause of justice. You will not shirk your task, no matter what obstacles you encounter. 
## Faith 
You are an adherent of a deity of law, justice, guardians or some other ethos that demands you protect the weak and punish the guilty. The gods have set you on your path of righteousness, and you shall not waver! You may be an ordained cleric or paladin, or simply a lay follower who possesses an abiding belief in their holy purpose. 
## Glory 
You adventure not for wealth or power, but for fame. You hunger to hear your name being chanted by adoring crowds, to be deluged in honours and titles, to have your path strewn with roses. You want to be loved and respected. Bards will sing of your greatness and your deeds will become legends. 
## Greed
You’re in this for the money. There’s gold in those dungeons and treasure in those vaults. You’ll solve any crime if the reward money is right. As long as the cash keeps flowing, you’ll keep on questing. You’re not necessarily amoral or obsessed with gold, but you are definitely focussed on base monetary rewards instead of some higher purpose.

You have watched kingdoms rise and fall, you have seen tyrants and monsters felled by heroes, and you have learned to identify fulcrum points and crises. The right intervention at the right time can push events one way or another. Bring down a vile sorcerer before he becomes too powerful, and you spare the world a century of suffering. ## Justice 
The world is cruel and unjust. The weak are trampled by the strong. The virtuous are brought down; liars and cheats are exalted and praised. Villainy triumphs, evil-doers prosper and the law is mocked and ignored – but no more! You are determined to be a force for justice and righteousness, to punish the guilty and protect the innocent. Perhaps you are devoted to the abstract principle of justice, or maybe some dark incident in your past drives you to uphold the law.
## Wanderlust 
You can’t stand to stay in the same place for long. You need to see new vistas and new faces. Adventuring gives you a reason to travel and a never-ending cavalcade of excitement and danger. You work hard to solve mysteries so you can move on to the next novelty. Home is the open road and the unmapped horizon. 

# Investigative Skills 
investiGative skiLLs are the Core of Gumshoe. These are the tools and techniques your characters use to uncover the clues you need to complete the adventure. Investigative Skills are broken into three categories – Cunning, Interpersonal and Lore. The chief purpose of these categories is to help the GM find appropriate skills quickly when creating an adventure or when improvising a scene. Some Investigative skills, like Search, will be used in almost every adventure. Others are called upon less frequently. When creating your character, strike a balance between the skills you rely on regularly and those that are only used occasionally, but are vitally needed when they are called on. Where a skill from the Pathfinder Roleplaying Game (like Bluff) has become an Investigative Skill, notes are given on how to use the skill in its new form outside of investigation.

> There are two sorts of clues. Some clues are immediately visible to everyone, but require expertise to interpret. You don’t need any skill to spot a plague-ridden corpse in the middle of the road, but it takes Medicine to tell that the corpse died of the gnome-pox. Other clues are easy to interpret, but require expertise to find. Search and Battlefield Scout are both useful in examining a battlefield or crime scene, but fulfil different roles. Battlefield Scout is used to discern the meaning behind obvious clues – what weapon was used, based on these wounds? How many attackers were there, based on their footprints and the blood splatters? What sort of enemy uses this ornate dagger? Search is used to find hidden clues, like a dropped coin or a secret door. Often, both skills will be needed to progress the plot.

####**tips for pLayers** 
If you’re unfamiliar with investigative games, here are a few tips to get started:  
- **If you’re stuck, look for clues:** If you don’t have a clear lead to follow, then you need more clues. Search around, try different skills, and find more information. Keep moving forward, and don’t be afraid to take risks. Inaction will never save the day. 
- **Clues aren’t everything:** A clue is just a piece of information; it’s up to you to interpret it and use it. Try to work out ways to use the information you discover.
- **Contain speculation:** Once you accumulate a number of clues, it’s tempting to engage in speculation and try to fit them into a coherent pattern. Beware of this temptation – it’s possible to get bogged down in speculation indefinitely. If a clue doesn’t fit, don’t stall the game by trying to come up with a theory that stretches to explain everything. Instead, search out more clues. The more information you have, the easier it is to discern the true shape of the mystery. Don’t speculate in lieu of investigating.

For NPCs who clearly deserve a rating in an investigative skill (Arcana for an archmage, Medicine for a renowned healer and so on), give a rating of 2-3 for an expert in the field, and 4 or more for a world-famous expert. 

### Using Investigative Skills 
Having even a single point in an Investigative skill means your character is fully trained in that field. Multiple points in an Investigative skill indicates exceptional mastery. No roll is ever needed to use an Investigative skill. 

### Clues 
The primary use of Investigative skills is to find clues. To find clues, inform the GM what you are doing and which skill you are using. This can be as specific or as general as you wish. You might say ‘I use Battlefield Scout to work out if the dead guard was killed by the same type of dagger as we saw earlier’ or ‘Can I learn anything with Local Knowledge here?’ If there is a core clue to be found, and you use the right skill, you will automatically find that clue. In other cases, the GM may give clues to characters with the right skill without prompting (‘Bob, with your expert knowledge of Engineering, you notice the tell-tale outline of a secret door’). 

Core CLues: Every investigative scene includes a core clue – a vitally important piece of information that enables you to further your investigation. A core clue might point the way to the next scene, or suggest a new line of inquiry, or throw new light on information you’ve already unearthed, but it will always let the investigation move onwards. Therefore, if a core clue is present, you will always find it. There is no need to spend points to find Core Clues – as long as you have the skill, you get the clue. For example, the characters are investigating a break-in at the secret hall of the thieves’ guild. The GM has determined that the core clue for the scene is the discovery of an antique dagger in a pile of recently stolen goods, suggesting that someone has been looting the tombs of the long-dead master thieves. Anyone searching the vaults with Battlefield Scout, Appraise or Search notices the out-of-place dagger, and can guess that it must have come from the vaults. Those long-dead thieves knew the secrets of the guild – could some necromancer have called up their spirits to interrogate them? 

other CLues: Other Clues may be present in a scene. Most of these clues can be found in the same manner as core clues, by using the right Investigative Skill in the right place. Others require spending points (see below). These clues don’t point the way to the next scene, but might: • Provide added information to help the players make deductions 
• Hint at the nature and abilities of the opposition 
• Shed additional light on other aspects of the case 
To continue the example of the break-in at the thieves’ guild, the History skill could be used to identify the dagger as belonging to Master Thief Vartok Gleam, who was poisoned 183 years ago. Search or Engineering could be used to confirm that whoever broke into the thieves’ guild did so by bypassing the defences, so it must have been an inside job. Interpersonal skills like Diplomacy, Intimidation and Sense Motive can be used to question the guild officials and determine if they know anything more about the crime. 

### Spends & Benefits 
Player characters may have the opportunity to spend points from their Investigative Skill pools to get added benefits. The GM may have noted chances to spend points in his scenario notes; the players may also come up with their own uses for skills. In general, spends fall into three categories. 

Additional Information: Some clues can be found easily; other clues require extra effort. By spending points, the character gets extra information – either new information, or more details on clues that have already been found. This information will never be absolutely necessary to solving the mystery, but it may help the players make sense of other clues or save them time later on. For example, History lets the characters identify the dagger as belonging to long-dead master Gleam. A one-point History spend gives the character more information – Master Glean was poisoned by rivals in the thieves’ guild during a feud some 150 years ago, and his tomb is reputed to be somewhere in the necropolis under the city. 

benefits: Spends can also provide benefits other than more information. The characters might be able to make new allies, find items or monetary rewards, make tasks easier or acquire some other edge by spending points. For example, spending a point of Charm might convince one of the thieves to help the characters by giving them a preferential rate for fencing any treasure they find during their investigation. 

spotLiGht time: Some spends provide no concrete benefit, but do enhance the character’s reputation and demonstrate his competence. A two-point Religion spend lets the character know the unspoken name of the God of Thieves, a secret that’s supposed to be known only to the high priests of that shadowy deity. If the character idly drops it in conversation, he will astonish and impress the thieves’ guild. 

faIled sPeNds: Spend points are never wasted. If a player tries to spend points when neither the GM nor any of the players can think of a suitable benefit in that context, then nothing happens and no points are lost.

### Boosts 
During character creation, players can nominate a number of Investigative Skills as Boosts. When spending from a Boosted skill, your pool increases by 1 point after getting the information or benefit at hand. For example, Grungr the Barbarian has Nature as a Boosted skill. While exploring a dungeon, the GM offers the players the opportunity to spend a point of Nature to realise that one patch of luminescent fungus spits poisonous spores. Grungr spends a point from his Nature pool, receives the information, and gains a point of Nature back again. 

### Refreshing Pools
 If an Investigative skill pool is exhausted, it is refreshed (restored to its original value) between adventures. The GM may include opportunities during an adventure to refresh specific pools at the cost of time or resources. For example, spending hours reading in a library may grant a refresh on a particular Lore skill, while buying rounds of drinks and carousing with the locals might restore Local Knowledge. Not every adventure will include chances to refresh, so players are advised to husband their points carefully.
